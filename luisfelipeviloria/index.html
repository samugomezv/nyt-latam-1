What I Cover

I write about art, music and culture across Latin America — the concerts and biennials, the street murals and the streaming algorithms, the moments when a song or a painting manages to say something a news article cannot. My beat is broad by design: from the gallery openings of Mexico City and São Paulo to the festival stages of Santiago and Medellín, I follow the cultural currents that run underneath the region's politics and economics. I am based in Madrid, which puts me at the crossroads between Latin America and Europe, and gives me a particular vantage point on how the region is perceived, consumed and misread from abroad.

My Background

I grew up in Caracas, in the neighborhood of Chacao, in a family where culture was taken seriously — my mother was a theatre director, my uncle a documentary filmmaker. I studied literature and journalism at the Universidad Simón Bolívar and spent my early career writing arts criticism for Venezuelan newspapers and magazines, at a time when Caracas still had a genuinely vibrant cultural life.

I moved to Madrid in 2013, as Venezuela's crisis made independent journalism increasingly difficult. Spain gave me a new city to understand and a new perspective on the one I had left. I began covering the Latin American diaspora arts scene in Europe — the Venezuelan painters in Berlin, the Colombian novelists in Barcelona, the cumbia collectives remaking themselves in Madrid — and realized that the best way to understand what the region was becoming was to watch what it exported. I joined The Times in 2017 and have since reported from festivals, museums and concert venues across fifteen countries. I cover everything from the commercial machinery of Latin pop to the slow, quiet world of contemporary Latin American visual art.

Journalistic Ethics

Arts journalism is not soft journalism. Behind every album rollout and gallery opening are decisions about money, power and whose stories get told. I approach culture coverage with the same rigour I would apply to any beat: I verify, I seek out dissenting voices, and I am honest when an event fails to live up to its ambitions. I write in Spanish and English, and I believe that cultural criticism loses something essential when it is translated too quickly into the language of commerce or politics — so I try, always, to start with the work itself.
